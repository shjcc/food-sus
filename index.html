What is Food Sustainability?

Food sustainability in a general sense illustrates a notion of minimizing our contribution to climate change and minimizing the amount of waste or damage caused during food production. Food sustainability represents an ongoing issue that has direct ties with key developments in history. Brought on as a response to the impact of industrialization and imperialism, food sustainability highlights the complex economic, societal, and environmental forces at play.

Agriculture contributes to an estimated 30% of our total global emissions.

Deforestation contributes to 70-80% of all our human water withdrawals.

Livestock represents a pressing issue to food sustainability as it not only plagues biodiversity but is the largest source of water pollution.

If these issues are not addressed by 2050, estimates indicate that our food production will not meet our population's demands. [1]

However, our future may be saved through a historic lens into the past.

Victory Gardens

Throughout World War I and II, the United States experienced rapid food shortages, a result from the reallocation of resources to supplement the war effort. This food shortage illustrated an opportunity for crisis response in a way that signified an evolution for food production and preservation. The Victory Gardens initiative encouraged individuals and community members to grow food on their own, a collective effort to reduce the pressures of commercial food production. Victory Gardens or the War Garden Campaign arose in 1917—organised by the US National War Garden Commission and was popularised through pamphlets and posters. This campaign represented a chance for American citizens to plant their own gardens and rely less on outsourcing their food from neighbouring allies as “America alone can produce more food than it did before”[2]. Through relying on local food production methods, the campaign inadvertently created a key historical moment for food sustainable history.

Posters and pamphlets like the one shown above, proved to be a great success as “there were millions of health-giving, home-loving gardens”[3]. A key takeaway from this campaign was the importance of locally produced food. Within a food sustainability context, the Victory Gardens highlighted the efficiency of community driven food production and preservation, along with the immense carbon footprint reduction associated with waste reduction through composting. Another key factor within a food sustainability context was the importance of resilience through biodiversity. The diverse crop choices signified an opportunity to buffer against food shortages through a local crop rotation. This meant that if meat or dairy was in short supply, individuals and communities could rely on their wheat and potato crops for nutrients. The legacy of Victory Gardens allows us to draw inspiration by building and supporting our community gardens, promoting a culture of sustainable agriculture and biodiversity. These traits not only provide the community with an abundant and local supply of food, but also addresses climate issues through green spaces and allows for an influx of organic and regenerative farming, a key weapon against food sustainability issues.

Lessons from History

Throughout history, the roles of gender have impacted the way in which food is produced, spread, and consumed. Many societies and cultures in history have held women responsible for the preparation and preservation of food through a division of labor. This expectance on women throughout history to be held accountable for food-related tasks has major implications for food sustainability. History highlights how Women and their involvement in food preparation and preservation have long been undervalued, creating a significant disconnect between food and sustainable food practices. Highlighting the historical role of women in food-related tasks presents an opportunity to not only appreciate, but to illustrate the need for equality within food sustainability. Underutilizing and undervaluing a significant portion of our population and their contribution to food sustainability simply because of gender only leads to further setbacks.

Food sustainability saw a major setback in the form of industrialization. This era saw a boost in machinery and manufacturing, on a major scale and utilized through factories and workshops. The ramifications of the industrial revolution—first introduced in the mid eighteenth century within Britian—represented a major shift in how food was produced and preserved. The introduction of large-scale machinery and manufacturing created an imbalance through abnormally high growth along with a disregard for waste. “The Industrial Revolution…has encroached seriously on the world's store of fertility” [4]. Aside from damaging the environment through waste, industrialization impacts food sustainability through the introduction of poor-quality food and increased waste products. We see this historically through a population that has been “fed on improperly grown food” [5]. Ultimately, industrialization represents a historical challenge for food sustainability through the imbalance created by manufacturing greed.

Modern Food Sustainability

Solutions to the challenges facing food sustainability present themselves throughout history. Using key lessons from historical challenges and case studies, can we apply the remedies in our modern world. One key solution as presented in the issues facing the Victory Gardens and the industrial revolution, is the importance of community. Self-sufficiency through community driven food production and preservation presents itself as one of the most effective methods to combat the issues facing food sustainability. As “The situation can only be saved by the community as a whole” [6], food sustainability driven communities provide a defense against the globalized industrial food waste plaguing our environment and population.
